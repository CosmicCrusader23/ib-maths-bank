<p>Candidates will have knowledge of the factors that contributed to independence in Mozambique in 1975. They should discuss both external factors and internal factors and draw a conclusion as to which of these factors played the more important role.</p>
<p><em>Indicative content</em></p>
<p><strong>External factors</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Candidates could mention the formation of the Mozambique Liberation Front (FRELIMO) by exiles who were determined to end Portuguese rule.</li>
<li>Support for the Mozambican independence movement from leaders of independent African nations such as Nyerere, who allowed them to use their countries for their military bases.</li>
<li>The morale and support given to FRELIMO by the World Council of Churches and the Vatican also played a role.</li>
<li>The Portuguese were also fighting nationalism in Guinea and Angola and it can be argued that this spread their resources too thinly.</li>
<li>The Organisation of African Unity (OAU) provided the opportunity for its members to offer military training and a supply of weapons. In 1965 the OAU recognized FRELIMO as the legitimate representative for Mozambique.</li>
<li>The collapse of the fascist state in Portugal in 1974 was a key factor in the decision to end the independence war and grant Mozambique its independence.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Internal factors</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Samora Machel and other Mozambicans were effective military leaders of FRELIMO in the struggle for independence.</li>
<li>FRELIMO enjoyed the widespread support of the local population and this provided it with more strength.</li>
<li>The use of guerilla tactics by FRELIMO fighters assisted in furthering Mozambique’s independence.</li>
<li>The discriminatory policies of the Portuguese fuelled internal opposition to its rule and bolstered FRELIMO.</li>
</ul>
<p><em>The above material is an indication of what candidates may elect to write about in their responses. However, the list is not exhaustive and <strong>no set answer is required</strong>.</em></p>
