<p>greater species diversity/greater population size usually lead to greater resilience;<br/>with more species, it is more likely others can take over the role/niche of any lost/declining species;<br/>more food chains/energy/biogeochemical pathways in an ecosystem provides redundancy therefore greater stability;<br/>a variety of species is more likely to include those resistant to environmental change;<br/>larger populations provide greater storages that can last over periods of lower productivity; larger populations generally carry greater genetic diversity;<br/>larger populations of invasive species may lead to reduced diversity/resilience;<br/>lower populations are more prone to extinction after a disturbance (e.g. habitat fragmentation) / or due to stochastic fluctuations;<br/>an ecosystem may be more resilient if there are many small populations of different species than one large population of a single dominating species;<br/>large populations of foundation/keystone species may be crucial for resilience of certain ecosystems (e.g. corals, kelp, beavers, elephants, pines, hemlock);</p>
<p><em><strong>Note</strong>: Award credit to any valid arguments providing they directly relate species diversity or population size to resilience.</em><br/><em>Award <strong>[3]</strong> max for responses addressing only diversity or only population size.</em></p>

<p><em>Similarities</em>:<br/>both may involve species identification;<br/>both may involve quantitative sampling/estimating the abundance of living organisms;<br/>both require multiple samples for effective comparisons/reliability;<br/>both indices involve evaluating the range/variety of different species;<br/>both involve calculating a single figure (index) from a collection of data;</p>
<p><em>Differences</em>:<br/>only biotic indices (not diversity indices) give different values to sensitive and tolerant species;<br/>biotic indices are specifically used for evaluating impact of pollution whereas diversity indices just measure variety and evenness of species/general condition/maturity of an ecosystem;<br/>diversity indices are appropriate for most ecosystems whereas biotic indices are usually for aquatic systems;<br/>biotic indices usually focus on animal species/macroinvertebrates alone whereas diversity indices can be used for plant or animal communities;<br/>diversity indices usually address all species present in system whereas biotic index focuses on a particular selection;</p>
<p><em><strong>Note</strong>: Award <strong>[4]</strong> max if only similarities or only differences are given.</em><br/><em>Only credit differences where both sides of the contrast are given or clearly implicit. No credit for naming indices eg Simpson’s, Trent etc.</em></p>
