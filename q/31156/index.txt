<p><em>Refer to the paper 1 section B assessment criteria when awarding marks</em>. <em>These can be found under the “Your tests” tab &gt; supplemental materials.</em> </p>
<p>The command term “discuss” requires candidates to offer a considered review of the influence of technology on cognitive processes.</p>
<p>Technology is a broad term that includes the use of computers, the Internet and social media, mobile phones, video games, and virtual reality.</p>
<p>Relevant studies may include, but are not limited to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Sparrow <em>et al</em>.’s (2011) study of transactive memory</li>
<li>Rosen <em>et al</em> (2011, 2013) on the effect of multi-tasking on memory recall</li>
<li>Mueller and Oppenheimer (2014) on computer assisted notetaking and memory</li>
<li>Storm <em>et al</em> (2016) on the Google effect</li>
<li>Blacker <em>et al</em> (2014) on the effect of video games on visual working memory</li>
<li>Swing <em>et al</em> (2010) on the effect of screen time on attention</li>
<li>Kaspersky Lab’s (2015) study on digital amnesia.</li>
</ul>
<p>Relevant discussion points may include, but are not limited to:</p>
<ul>
<li>The balance between ecological and internal validity in evaluating research</li>
<li>Biological explanations of why technology may affect cognitive processes</li>
<li>The difficulty of establishing causal relationships</li>
<li>Issues of researcher bias</li>
<li>Linking research to cognitive models of processes - <em>e.g.</em> the working memory model</li>
<li>Researcher and participant biases</li>
</ul>
<p>Candidates may discuss the effect of technology on one cognitive process to demonstrate depth of knowledge, or discuss the effects on more than one cognitive process to demonstrate breadth of knowledge. Both approaches are equally acceptable.</p>
