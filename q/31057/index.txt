<p><em>Refer to the paper 2 assessment criteria when awarding marks</em>.</p>
<p>The command term “discuss” requires candidates to offer a considered review that includes various concepts of normality and abnormality.</p>
<p>Concepts of normality and abnormality may include, but are not limited to:<br/>• the mental health criterion/model<br/>• the statistical criterion/model<br/>• abnormality as mental illness (medical model)<br/>• the psychoanalytic explanation of the concept of abnormality<br/>• the cognitive explanation of the concept of abnormality<br/>• deviation from social and cultural norms.</p>
<p>Discussion may include, but is not limited to:<br/>• cross-cultural issues<br/>• gender biases<br/>• supporting or contradicting evidence<br/>• the issue of labelling<br/>• changing norms on perceptions of normality (for example, changing views on homosexuality or political dissent)<br/>• difficulties in defining normality/abnormality<br/>• difficulties in diagnosing normality/abnormality.</p>
<p>Relevant research may include, but is not limited to:<br/>• Rosenhan and Seligman (1984) – seven criteria of abnormality<br/>• Jahoda (1958) – six characteristics of mental health<br/>• Szasz (1962) – mental disorders as “problems in living”<br/>• Bolton (1999) – cultural issues in overdiagnosis.</p>
<p>Although studies illustrating difficulty in diagnosis (<em>eg</em> Rosenhan) may be marginally relevant to the question, the response must be focused on the broader issue of normality versus abnormality in order to be awarded the full range of marks.</p>
<p>Candidates may discuss a small number of explanations of normality and abnormality in order to demonstrate depth of knowledge, or may discuss a larger number of explanations of normality and abnormality in order to demonstrate breadth of knowledge. Both approaches are equally acceptable.</p>
