<p>resilience is the ability to withstand disturbances / tendency to maintain stability/avoid tipping points; <br/>generally, biomes with higher primary productivity (e.g. rainforests/estuaries/wetlands) are more resilient than those with lower productivity (e.g. tundra/deserts); <br/>more productive biomes can support more species/diversity; <br/>diversity increases resilience because loss of one species is more easily replaced by others; <br/>more productive biomes support more branching food chains / greater complexity of interrelationships; <br/>…that allows for more negative feedback mechanisms/shifting feeding habits maintaining stability/providing more resilience; <br/>more productive biomes produce larger biotic storages; <br/>larger storages are less likely to be eliminated/reduced beyond a tipping point so contribute to greater resilience; <br/>larger storages provide higher maximum sustainable yields so are less prone to overharvesting; <br/>higher productivity entails faster plant growth, thus more effective regeneration after a disturbance; <br/>oceanic biomes have low productivity per unit area but their large size increases their resilience; <br/>coral reefs have high productivity but narrow niche requirements give them low resilience;</p>
<p><em>Allow credit for valid counterexamples as in last two MPs</em>.</p>
