<p style="text-align: center;"><strong>Figure 8(a): Conservation within the Coral Triangle</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>The Coral Triangle Initiative on Coral Reefs, Fisheries and Food Security (CTI-CFF) is an agreement between all six nations in the area with the objectives of:<br/>
<ul>
<li style="padding-left: 30px;">ensuring food security and a sustainable livelihood of residents of the Coral Triangle</li>
<li style="padding-left: 30px;">protecting the unique ecosystem and marine species within the Coral Triangle.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Conservation projects in the Coral Triangle involve collaboration between national governments, regional organisations, non-governmental organisations, private companies and local communities.</li>
<li>Popular approaches to encourage sustainable use of the sea and its resources include creation of Marine Protected Areas (MPAs) and networks of Locally Managed Marine Areas (LMMAs).</li>
<li>MPAs are usually managed by national and local government whereas LMMAs are more likely to be managed by the local community.</li>
<li>These designated areas are often classified according to their use or the restrictions placed on them, such as “no-take zones” where no fishing is allowed or “tourism zones” that can be used for recreation but where other activities such as fishing are banned.</li>
<li>Some zones allow restricted activity, such as use by local fishermen using only traditional fishing methods or during certain times of the year.</li>
<li>Research has shown that over time in a well-managed MPA there is an increase in fish size, fish numbers and overall biodiversity.</li>
<li>There are currently over 1000 MPAs within the Coral Triangle.</li>
<li>The Coral Triangle Initiative aims to designate 20 % of each of the main habitats within the Coral Triangle as MPAs by 2020.</li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: center;">[Source: adapted from www.coraltriangleinitiative.org; http://wwf.panda.org]</p>
